Strong Gravitational Lensing

What is strong lensing

Imagine looking through a wine glass and seeing a distorted view of the room behind it. The glass deflects light rays emanating from background objects, producing the distorted image you see. As it turns out, gravity can also deflect light rays, and a very massive object like a galaxy can produce distorted images of luminous background sources, like a giant cosmic wine glass. This effect is called strong gravitational lensing, and it is a useful tool in astrophysics since it allows us to study the mysterious dark matter that fills our universe through its gravitational lensing effects.
<>br /> As an example, check out the image on the far left of the nearby galaxy Andromeda. The central image is what Andromeda would like like if it were strongly lensed by another galaxy.
On the far right, I've made an animation that shows what happens when a very dense, very massive blob of dark matter moves through the lensed arc. Although we can't directly detect this blob of dark matter, we can infer its presence by the distortion it produces in the lensed arc.

Image Magnifications in Quadrupoly Imaged Quasars

When the background object is very bright but more compact than a galaxy, rather than observing a lensed arc you might see two or four duplicate images of it. The magnifications of these images are extremely sensitive to the presence of very small blobs of dark matter.
